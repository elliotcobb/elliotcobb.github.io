Not eating vegetables

Do you remember the glory of Pizza lunchables? I do. Remember being so young you didn't know how to bathe, but still making your own Pizza? Here you were, king of your own pie, belle of your own pizza ball.

Once you consider the ingretients, though, it doesn't seem so glorious. Once you peel back the plastic film, there are no hints of fresh basil or roasted tomatoes. It wasn't spicy because there were no spices. In fact it was sweet because the sauce was ketchup. It's room temperature, it's essentially a cracker crust, It's really more like you're eating a photo of a piece of pizza.

But hey you've got to know your customer, and let's face it kids have boring tastes. For some reason all the creativity and chaos they bring to their art and play gets left at the cafeteria door. They'll put anything in their mouths except if you tell them it's food. But I can't blame them. they're new to this world and I guess if you're 7, communion wafer Pizza is still quite exciting. Maybe the sad thing is that us adults can't find pleasure in the little things anymore. We have to eat okonomiyaki just to feel something.

Maybe lunchables are more so a reflection of how little time and creativity adults have that they're willing to send their kids to school with military rations.

One thing kids aren't picky about is PB&j. and the Lunchables of PB&j is another made-up adjective turned lunch product called Uncrustables, from the good people at Smuckers. The Smuckers Uncrustable is essentially a PBJ embryo wrapped in a thin film of melt in your mouth white bread. The hard thing about the uncrustable is that the peanut butter and the jelly were never completely mixed. It's like a yin yang symbol in there. and unless you held it up to the light like a film negative you're probably going to get an entire mouthful of dry peanut butter or a jelly shot.

Still, I love uncrustables. And I bring them forth for your consideration because they're not any worse than a normal PB&j sandwich. Uncrustables are like if you go to Grandma's house expecting lasagna and she makes Stouffer's. It's not half bad. But a pizza lunchable is like showing up for pasta night and Grandma serves cup of noodles. It really leaves something to be desired.



This lecture was brought to you by Kraft Heinz and the good people at Smuckers.

True story about Mimi.

My grandma Mimi is 96 years old, and we don't know how she got to be so old since, I swear to you, she does not eat vegetables. Tomatoes, no. Broccoli, no. Asparagus, no. Mushrooms, no. Onions and garlic, no. Maybe she knows something we don't. But I think her secret is that she's always thinking positive. Sometimes a little too positive. Like at Thanksgiving last year she was saying how no one in the family ever gets fat. Which is true, but apparently it's not because we eat vegetables. And then here's the part where she maybe takes the positive thinking a little too far. See if you can spot it.

Grandma Mimi: "In the Cobb family no one really gets fat"

Dad: "Well, there was uncle Karl."

Grandma Mimi: "Well, he lost the weight..."

Dad: "Mom, he had cancer."

Did you catch that? Mimi took the late Uncle Karl's bout with cancer to be a somewhat positive occurance seeing as he did lose weight. Maybe a bit of a stretch.

Love Mimi dearly, and I hope if I live to 96 I can also manage to do it solely on chicken alfredo. As my friend Micah says, that's a flex.
